# Salary Negotiation & Offer Evaluation Guide

> For transitioning into AI Engineer roles (Full-Stack AI, Applied AI, LLM Engineer)

---

## Part 1: Know Your Market Value

### Research Salary Ranges

**Where to look:**
1. **levels.fyi** (most accurate for tech)
   - Search: "AI Engineer", "Machine Learning Engineer", "Applied AI Engineer"
   - Filter by: Company, location, years of experience
   - **Your profile**: 8 years total experience, but entering AI → likely "Mid to Senior" (L4-L5 at big tech)

2. **Glassdoor**
   - Company-specific data
   - Less reliable than levels.fyi but good for smaller companies

3. **Blind** (anonymous tech community)
   - Real salary data from verified employees
   - Good for understanding comp structure

4. **AngelList / Wellfound**
   - Startup salary + equity ranges
   - Equity is higher, base is lower than big tech

**Typical Ranges (2024, USD, US-based):**

| Role | Experience | Base Salary | Total Comp (incl. equity) |
|------|------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **AI Engineer (Startup)** | Mid-level | $120K - $160K | $140K - $200K |
| **AI Engineer (Big Tech)** | L4/Mid | $150K - $200K | $250K - $400K |
| **Senior AI Engineer (Startup)** | Senior | $160K - $220K | $200K - $300K |
| **Senior AI Engineer (Big Tech)** | L5 | $180K - $250K | $350K - $550K |

**Your positioning:**
- **Strengths**: 8 years engineering, tech lead experience, proven system design
- **Weakness**: Less direct AI experience
- **Target**: Mid-level AI Engineer roles at **senior-level base salary** (your leadership experience offsets AI newness)
- **Realistic range**: $140K - $180K base (startup), $160K - $200K base (mid-size), $180K - $220K (big tech if you get L4/L5)

## Part 2: Understanding Total Compensation

### Compensation Structure

**Total Compensation = Base + Equity + Bonus + Benefits**

#### 1. Base Salary
- **What it is**: Guaranteed cash paid bi-weekly/monthly
- **Why it matters**: Used for mortgage applications, guaranteed income
- **Negotiable?**: **YES**, most negotiable component

#### 2. Equity (Stock Options or RSUs)
- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)**: Common at public companies (Google, Meta, etc.)
  - You get actual shares on a vesting schedule (usually 4 years)
  - Example: $100K RSU grant vests 25% per year for 4 years
  - Taxed as income when vested
  - Value fluctuates with stock price

- **Stock Options**: Common at startups
  - You get the *right* to buy shares at a strike price
  - Example: 20,000 options at $1 strike price, company valued at $5/share = $80K paper value
  - Only worth something if company exits or goes public
  - **90% of startup equity is worth $0**—be realistic

- **Negotiable?**: **SOMETIMES** (easier at startups, harder at big tech with fixed bands)

#### 3. Annual Bonus
- **Performance bonus**: 10-20% of base (common)
- **Signing bonus**: One-time payment in year 1 (negotiable!)
- Example: $150K base + 15% bonus = $172.5K cash in year 1

#### 4. Benefits
- **Health insurance**: Can be worth $10K-$20K/year
- **401(k) match**: 3-6% match = free money
- **PTO/Vacation**: Unlimited PTO often means *less* time off (beware)
- **Learning stipend**: $1K-$5K/year for courses, conferences
- **Remote work**: Huge quality-of-life benefit

## Part 3: Evaluating an Offer

### The Offer Evaluation Framework

**When you get an offer, score it across 5 dimensions:**

| Dimension | Weight | Questions to Ask |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **Compensation** | 30% | Is the total comp competitive? Cash vs equity split? |
| **Growth** | 25% | Will I learn? Is there a clear path to senior/staff? |
| **Team & Culture** | 20% | Do I respect my manager? Is the team collaborative? |
| **Product & Impact** | 15% | Is the product meaningful? Am I building or maintaining? |
| **Work-Life Balance** | 10% | Hours expected? On-call? Remote flexibility? |

**Example Scoring:**

**Offer A (Startup):**
- Comp: $140K base + $40K equity (paper value) = $180K → **7/10** (equity risky)
- Growth: Founding AI engineer, will own systems → **9/10**
- Team: 5-person eng team, CEO is non-technical → **6/10**
- Product: AI legal assistant (meaningful!) → **8/10**
- Balance: Startup hours (50-60/week expected) → **5/10**
- **Total: 7.0/10**

**Offer B (Mid-Size Tech):**
- Comp: $170K base + $30K RSU/year = $200K → **9/10**
- Growth: Join existing AI team, mentorship available → **7/10**
- Team: 15-person team, experienced manager → **8/10**
- Product: Adding AI features to existing SaaS → **6/10** (less exciting)
- Balance: 40-45 hours/week, flexible remote → **9/10**
- **Total: 7.9/10**

**Decision**: Offer B (better balance, more realistic comp)

## Part 4: The Negotiation Process

### Timeline & Strategy

**Step 1: Receive the Offer (Day 0)**
- They'll call or email with offer details
- **DO NOT accept immediately** (even if it's great)
- Say: *"Thank you! This is exciting. Can I have this in writing and a few days to review?"*
- **Get the written offer** (base, equity, bonus, benefits)

**Step 2: Evaluate (Days 1-2)**
- Compare against market data (levels.fyi)
- Calculate total comp over 4 years
- List your concerns (is base too low? equity structure unclear?)

**Step 3: Negotiate (Days 3-5)**
- Call or email your recruiter
- Be specific about what you want
- **Justify** with data or competing offers

**Step 4: Close (Days 6-7)**
- They'll come back with updated offer or explanation
- Either accept or decline
- **Don't negotiate more than twice** (risk annoying them)

---

### What You Can Negotiate

| Component | Negotiability | Strategy |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Base Salary** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | ALWAYS negotiate this. Ask for 10-20% more than initial offer. |
| **Signing Bonus** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Easy to negotiate (one-time cost for them). Useful if base is capped. |
| **Equity** | ⭐⭐⭐ | Negotiable at startups, harder at big tech (fixed bands). |
| **Start Date** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Easy to negotiate (more time to wrap up current role). |
| **Title** | ⭐⭐ | Harder, but possible if you're between levels. |
| **Benefits** | ⭐ | Usually fixed, but you can ask (extra PTO, learning budget). |

---

### Negotiation Scripts

#### Script 1: Ask for Higher Base (Market Data)
> "Thank you for the offer! I'm excited about the role and the team. 
>
> I've been researching market rates for AI Engineers with my experience (8 years full-stack + system design), and I'm seeing ranges of $X - $Y for similar roles [cite levels.fyi or Glassdoor].
>
> Given my background in leading teams and architecting production systems, I was hoping we could discuss a base salary closer to $[X+15%]. Is there flexibility here?"

#### Script 2: Ask for Signing Bonus (When Base is Capped)
> "I understand the base salary is at the top of the band for this level. 
>
> Would it be possible to include a signing bonus to help bridge the gap? Given my experience and the value I'll bring to the team, I was thinking $[15K-30K] would be fair."

#### Script 3: Leverage Another Offer (Only if TRUE!)
> "I want to be transparent—I have another offer with a total comp of $[X], but your company is my top choice because [specific reason: team, product, mission].
>
> Is there any flexibility on the base or equity to help me make this decision easier?"

#### Script 4: Ask for More Equity (Startups)
> "I'm excited about the company's growth potential. Given the risk of joining at this stage, would it be possible to increase the equity grant? I was hoping for closer to [X]% or [Y] more options."

#### Script 5: Ask About Refreshers (Multi-Year Comp)
> "The offer looks great for year 1. Can you walk me through what compensation looks like in years 2-4? Are there annual equity refreshers or performance bonuses?"

---

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Accepting the first offer** → They expect you to negotiate  
❌ **Saying "I need $X to live"** → They don't care about your personal finances  
❌ **Lying about competing offers** → They might call your bluff  
❌ **Negotiating over email only** → Have a conversation first, follow up in writing  
❌ **Being aggressive or entitled** → Stay collaborative: "I'm excited, and I'm hoping we can find a number that works for both of us"  
❌ **Negotiating every tiny thing** → Pick 1-2 things that matter most  
❌ **Not getting it in writing** → Verbal offers don't count

## Part 5: Equity Deep Dive

### Understanding Startup Equity

**Your offer says: "20,000 stock options"**

**Questions to ask:**
1. **What's the strike price?** (Price you pay to exercise)
   - Example: $1/share

2. **What's the current valuation?** (What's the company worth today?)
   - Example: $50M valuation, 10M shares outstanding = $5/share current price

3. **What's your ownership percentage?**
   - 20,000 options / 10,000,000 shares = **0.2%**

4. **What's the vesting schedule?**
   - Standard: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff
   - You get 0 shares in year 1, then 25% at 1 year, then monthly vesting after

5. **What happens if the company is acquired?**
   - Single-trigger acceleration? (You get all shares immediately)
   - Double-trigger? (You only get shares if you're terminated post-acquisition)

**Realistic outcome calculation:**
- **Scenario 1 (Failure)**: Company shuts down → Equity = $0 (80% of startups)
- **Scenario 2 (Modest Exit)**: Acquired for $100M → Your 0.2% = $200K (before taxes, dilution)
- **Scenario 3 (Success)**: IPO at $1B valuation → Your 0.2% = $2M (before taxes, dilution)
- **Scenario 4 (Unicorn)**: IPO at $10B → Your 0.2% = $20M

**Dilution**: Each funding round dilutes your ownership (0.2% → 0.15% → 0.1%...)

**Tax trap**: When you exercise options, you pay taxes on the spread (current price - strike price) even if you can't sell.

### RSUs vs Options: Which is Better?

| Feature | RSUs (Big Tech) | Stock Options (Startup) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| **Risk** | Low (stock has value today) | High (might be worth $0) |
| **Tax** | Taxed as income when vested | Taxed when exercised + when sold |
| **Liquidity** | Can sell immediately (if vested) | Can't sell until exit/IPO |
| **Upside** | Limited (stock already public) | Unlimited (if company 10x's) |
| **Best for** | Stability, guaranteed comp | Risk-takers, high upside seekers |

**Example:**
- **Offer A**: $150K base + $50K RSU/year (big tech) = **$200K guaranteed** (RSUs vest to real shares)
- **Offer B**: $140K base + $100K options (startup) = **$140K guaranteed + lottery ticket**

**Which to choose?**
- **If you need money now**: Take RSUs (guaranteed value)
- **If you're young, risk-tolerant**: Take options (potential 10-100x upside)
- **If you're transitioning careers**: Take RSUs (safer bet while you're learning)

### Checklist: What to Compare

| Benefit | Company A | Company B | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Health Insurance** | Premium: $50/month | Premium: $200/month | Company A saves you $1,800/year |

**Hidden value calculation:**
- Company A benefits are worth ~$17K/year more than B
- So if A offers $155K and B offers $170K, **real comp is similar**

## Part 7: Red Flags to Watch For

### During Negotiation
🚩 **"This is our final offer, take it or leave it"** → Aggressive, inflexible culture  
🚩 **Recruiter gets defensive when you negotiate** → They don't value your experience  
🚩 **Offer expires in 24-48 hours** → Pressure tactic (unless it's a true deadline)  
🚩 **Equity details are vague** → "We'll figure it out later" = bad sign  

### In the Offer Letter
🚩 **"At-will employment" + no severance** → You can be fired anytime with no payout (standard in US, but negotiate severance if senior)  
🚩 **Non-compete clause (1+ years)** → Limits your future options (may be unenforceable)  
🚩 **IP assignment clause** → Company owns everything you build (even side projects)  
🚩 **Vague equity details** → Should state exact number of shares, strike price, vesting schedule

## Part 8: After You Accept

### Onboarding & Year 1
- **Deliver early wins** (first 90 days matter for performance reviews)
- **Document your impact** (keep a "brag doc" for raise/promo discussions)
- **Build relationships** (political capital helps in raise negotiations)

### Asking for a Raise (Year 1-2)
**When**: Performance review cycle (usually annual)

**How**: Present a case with data
> "I've been here for 1 year and delivered [X impact: shipped Y feature, improved Z metric]. I've researched market rates and see that similar roles are paying $[X]. I'd like to discuss bringing my compensation to $[Y], which reflects my contributions and market value."

**Expected raise**: 5-10% annually (without promo)  
**With promotion**: 15-30% bump

## Key Takeaways
1. **Research market rates** (levels.fyi, Glassdoor) before negotiating
2. **Total comp = Base + Equity + Bonus + Benefits** (don't just look at base)
3. **ALWAYS negotiate** (they expect it, worst case they say no)
4. **Get competing offers** if possible (best leverage)
5. **Evaluate equity realistically** (most startup equity = $0)
6. **Calculate benefits value** (health insurance, 401(k), PTO add up)
7. **Don't accept immediately** (even if excited—take 3-5 days)
8. **Be collaborative, not adversarial** ("I'm excited, let's find a number that works")
9. **Get everything in writing** (verbal offers don't count)
10. **Know your walk-away number** (don't take a bad deal just to switch careers)
